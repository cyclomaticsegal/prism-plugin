# Utilitarianism — John Stuart Mill

> Excerpt from Utilitarianism, published 1863. Public domain.

Mill sets out to defend the utilitarian theory of ethics against its critics and to refine the account offered by his predecessor Jeremy Bentham. The creed which accepts as the foundation of morals utility, or the greatest happiness principle, holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness. By happiness is intended pleasure and the absence of pain; by unhappiness, pain and the privation of pleasure.

Mill immediately addresses the most common objection: that utilitarianism is a doctrine worthy only of swine, reducing human morality to the pursuit of animal pleasures. His response introduces the crucial distinction between higher and lower pleasures. It is quite compatible with the principle of utility to recognise the fact that some kinds of pleasure are more desirable and more valuable than others. The pleasures of the intellect, of the feelings and imagination, and of the moral sentiments have a higher value than those of mere sensation. Of two pleasures, if there be one to which all or almost all who have experience of both give a decided preference, irrespective of any feeling of moral obligation to prefer it, that is the more desirable pleasure.

The test is the verdict of competent judges — those who have experienced both kinds of pleasure. It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied. If the fool or the pig is of a different opinion, it is because they know only their own side of the question. The other party to the comparison knows both sides. No intelligent human being would consent to be a fool even if persuaded that the fool was perfectly content with his lot.

Mill distinguishes the standard of morality from its motivation. The utilitarian standard is not the agent's own greatest happiness but the greatest amount of happiness altogether. The standard demands strict impartiality: between one's own happiness and that of another, the utilitarian is required to be as strictly impartial as a disinterested and benevolent spectator. This is the true content of the golden rule and the complete spirit of the ethics of utility.

On the question of proof, Mill acknowledges that first principles are not susceptible of direct proof in the strict logical sense. One cannot deduce the desirability of happiness from prior premises. But he argues that the sole evidence it is possible to produce that anything is desirable is that people do actually desire it. Each person, so far as he believes it to be attainable, desires his own happiness. This being a fact, we have all the proof which the case admits of, and all which it is possible to require, that happiness is a good — that each person's happiness is a good to that person, and the general happiness therefore a good to the aggregate of all persons.

Mill addresses the relationship between justice and utility, which he considers the strongest challenge to utilitarianism. The sentiment of justice feels qualitatively different from ordinary calculations of expediency. It carries a feeling of moral compulsion, a sense that violations deserve punishment. Mill argues that this powerful sentiment is explicable within utilitarian terms. Justice concerns those moral rules that are most essential to human well-being — the rules protecting security of person and property, fair dealing, and the keeping of promises. Because these rules are so vital, the feelings attached to their observance are correspondingly intense. But the ultimate foundation remains utility: justice is the name for certain classes of moral rules which concern the essentials of human well-being more nearly, and are therefore of more absolute obligation, than any other rules for the guidance of life.

The utilitarian must sometimes weigh competing utilities, and Mill concedes that in particular cases reasonable people may disagree. But this difficulty afflicts every moral system, not only utilitarianism. The advantage of the utilitarian principle is that it provides a single, intelligible standard against which competing claims can be measured.
