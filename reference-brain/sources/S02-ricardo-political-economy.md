# Principles of Political Economy and Taxation — David Ricardo

> Excerpt from On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation, published 1817. Public domain.

Ricardo's central project is to determine the laws that regulate the distribution of the produce of the earth among three classes of the community: the proprietor of the land, the owner of capital, and the labourer. He regards this as the principal problem in political economy, and his analysis of value and rent provides the framework for understanding how wealth flows among these groups.

On the theory of value, Ricardo builds upon but departs from Adam Smith. The value of a commodity, he argues, depends on the relative quantity of labour that is necessary for its production, and not on the greater or less compensation that is paid for that labour. Two goods that require equal amounts of labour to produce will exchange at equal rates, regardless of whether the labourers who produced them were well or poorly paid. This labour theory of value applies to all reproducible goods — those commodities whose supply can be increased by human industry. For rare and non-reproducible goods such as statues, paintings, or wines of peculiar quality, value is determined entirely by scarcity and the desires of those who wish to possess them.

Ricardo acknowledges that capital employed in production complicates the picture. When durable machinery or tools are used, the value of the product reflects not only the labour directly applied but also the labour previously expended in creating the capital. Differences in the durability and proportion of fixed versus circulating capital across industries create apparent deviations from the pure labour theory, but Ricardo maintains that labour remains the fundamental regulator of value.

On the subject of rent, Ricardo develops what became known as the Ricardian theory of rent. Rent is that portion of the produce of the earth which is paid to the landlord for the use of the original and indestructible powers of the soil. It arises not because land is naturally productive but because land varies in fertility and location. When population growth and rising demand for food compel the cultivation of inferior lands, the superior lands command a rent equal to the difference in their productive advantage.

In a new country with abundant fertile land, no rent would be paid, since no one would pay for the use of land when equally good land was freely available. Only when second-quality land is brought into cultivation does first-quality land begin to yield rent, and the rent equals the difference in output between the two grades. As population continues to grow and yet more inferior lands are cultivated, the rent on all superior grades rises correspondingly. Rent is therefore not a cause of high prices but a consequence of them. Corn is not dear because rent is paid; rather, rent is paid because corn is dear and because the least productive land in cultivation sets the price.

This framework has profound implications for the distribution of wealth. As a nation grows and is obliged to resort to poorer soils, a larger share of the total produce goes to landlords as rent, while profits on capital tend to fall. Wages are governed by the cost of the necessaries required to sustain the labouring population. The interests of the landlord are thus opposed to those of every other class in the community, since landlords benefit from the very scarcity and difficulty that raises costs for everyone else.

Ricardo's analysis of comparative advantage in trade extends these principles internationally, showing that even when one nation is more efficient at producing everything, both nations benefit from specializing in the goods they produce at relatively lower cost. This insight remains one of the most powerful and counterintuitive results in all of economics, demonstrating that trade creates wealth not through absolute advantage but through the efficient allocation of each nation's limited labour and capital.
