# Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems — Galileo Galilei

> Excerpt from Dialogo sopra i due massimi sistemi del mondo, published 1632. Public domain.

Galileo presents his argument in the form of a four-day conversation among three characters: Salviati, who speaks for the Copernican system and represents Galileo's own views; Sagredo, an intelligent layman open to persuasion; and Simplicio, a defender of Aristotelian and Ptolemaic orthodoxy. The dramatic form allows Galileo to advance radical ideas while formally maintaining a posture of impartial inquiry — a posture the Inquisition would ultimately see through.

The fundamental question is whether the earth stands motionless at the centre of the universe, as Aristotle and Ptolemy taught, or whether it rotates on its axis daily and revolves around the sun annually, as Copernicus proposed. Simplicio defends the traditional view with the arguments of common sense and authority. We do not feel the earth move. If it rotated, a stone dropped from a tower would land far from its base, since the earth would have moved out from under it during the fall. Birds in flight would be left behind by the spinning ground beneath them.

Salviati dismantles these objections by developing the principle of the relativity of motion. A stone dropped from the mast of a moving ship falls at the base of the mast, not behind it, because the stone shares the forward motion of the ship. The same principle applies to objects on a rotating earth. Everything on the earth's surface — stones, birds, clouds, the air itself — shares the earth's motion, and therefore that motion is imperceptible to those participating in it. You cannot determine whether you are in motion or at rest by observing phenomena within a closed system. In a cabin below decks on a smoothly sailing ship, no experiment you perform — water dripping, fish swimming, butterflies flying — will reveal whether the ship moves or stands still. This is Galileo's principle of relativity, and it removes the most intuitive objections to a moving earth.

Galileo turns the argument from simplicity against the Ptolemaic system. To account for the observed motions of the planets, the Ptolemaic system requires a staggeringly complex apparatus of epicycles, deferents, and equants. The entire celestial sphere, with all its stars, must revolve around the earth once every twenty-four hours — a motion of inconceivable speed for objects at such distances. The Copernican system replaces all of this with a single rotation of the relatively small earth on its own axis and the orbital motion of the planets around the sun. Nature does not multiply things unnecessarily, and the simpler explanation is to be preferred where it accounts for the same phenomena.

Galileo presents the evidence from his telescopic observations: the phases of Venus, which are inexplicable on the Ptolemaic model but naturally explained by the Copernican; the moons of Jupiter, which demonstrate that not everything orbits the earth; the mountains and craters of the moon, which destroy the Aristotelian doctrine of celestial perfection; and the sunspots, which show that even the sun is subject to change.

On the fourth day, Galileo presents his theory of the tides as evidence for the earth's motion, arguing that the combination of the earth's rotation and revolution produces the oscillation of ocean waters. Though this particular argument was incorrect — tides are caused primarily by gravitational forces, as Newton would later show — the Dialogue as a whole constitutes one of the most powerful and consequential works in the history of science, demonstrating through patient reasoning and empirical evidence that the common-sense view of a stationary earth cannot withstand scrutiny.
