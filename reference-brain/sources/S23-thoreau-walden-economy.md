# Walden — Henry David Thoreau

> Excerpt from Walden; or, Life in the Woods, published 1854. Public domain.

Thoreau opens Walden with the chapter titled "Economy," in which he provides both a literal and philosophical accounting of his experiment in deliberate living at Walden Pond. He went to the woods because he wished to live deliberately, to front only the essential facts of life, and see if he could not learn what it had to teach, and not, when he came to die, discover that he had not lived.

The central observation is that the mass of men lead lives of quiet desperation. They labour ceaselessly to maintain standards of material comfort that are not merely unnecessary but actively corrosive to the life of the mind and spirit. What is called a good life is in fact a system of voluntary servitude in which men work six days a week to pay for houses they do not need, clothes that serve vanity rather than warmth, and food more elaborate than nutrition requires. The cost of a thing, Thoreau insists, is the amount of what he calls life which is required to be exchanged for it, immediately or in the long run.

Thoreau itemizes the true necessaries of life — food, shelter, clothing, and fuel — and demonstrates that they can be obtained with remarkably little labour. He built his cabin at Walden Pond for twenty-eight dollars and twelve cents, using mostly recycled materials and his own hands. He fed himself through a small bean-field, supplemented by foraging and occasional purchases. His total expenses for eight months at the pond amounted to a trifle. He supported himself by working roughly six weeks per year, leaving the remainder of his time free for reading, writing, walking, and observation.

The point is not that everyone should live in a cabin by a pond. It is that the relationship between labour and necessity has been grotesquely distorted by custom and social pressure. Men have become the tools of their tools. The farmer who owns a large farm is in fact owned by it — he cannot leave, cannot rest, cannot think, because the farm demands constant attention and its mortgage demands constant payment. The labourer in the factory has sold his days for wages that barely cover the cost of the life required to keep him working. In both cases, the supposed beneficiary of civilized economy is its prisoner.

Thoreau challenges the assumption that technological progress and economic growth represent genuine improvement. The railroad and the telegraph enable men to move and communicate faster, but faster to what purpose? We are in great haste to construct a magnetic telegraph from Maine to Texas, but Maine and Texas, it may be, have nothing important to communicate. The apparatus of modern life — its institutions, its machinery, its ever-proliferating comforts — is largely devoted to solving problems that it has itself created.

He proposes instead the principle of simplicity. Simplify, simplify. Instead of three meals a day, if it be necessary eat but one. Instead of a hundred dishes, five. Reduce the denominators of life to their lowest terms. The liberation that results is not deprivation but abundance — abundance of time, attention, and inner freedom. A man is rich in proportion to the number of things he can afford to let alone.

Thoreau's economy is not primarily financial but existential. The question is not how to earn more but how to need less, and thereby to reclaim the hours and energies that constitute an actual human life. He demonstrates by personal experiment that the barrier to this reclamation is not material necessity but mental habit — the unexamined assumption that the customs of one's neighbours define the boundaries of the possible.
