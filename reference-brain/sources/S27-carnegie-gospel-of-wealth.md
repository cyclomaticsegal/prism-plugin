# The Gospel of Wealth — Andrew Carnegie

> Excerpt from The Gospel of Wealth, published 1889. Public domain.

Carnegie begins by observing that the conditions of human life have been revolutionized within the past few hundred years. In former days, there was little difference between the dwelling, dress, food, and environment of the chief and those of his retainers. The contrast between the palace of the millionaire and the cottage of the labourer today measures the change which has come with civilization. This change, Carnegie insists, is not to be deplored but celebrated. The price society pays for the law of competition is great, but the advantages are greater still. It is to this law that we owe our wonderful material development, and while the law may sometimes be hard for the individual, it is best for the race, because it ensures the survival of the fittest in every department.

The central problem Carnegie addresses is what should be done with surplus wealth — the vast fortunes accumulated by those who have succeeded in the competitive struggle. He identifies three modes of disposing of such wealth. First, it may be left to the families of the decedents. Carnegie regards this as misguided. Great sums bequeathed to children generally do more harm than good, encouraging idleness and dissipation rather than productive effort. The parent who leaves his son enormous wealth generally deadens the talents and energies of the son and tempts him to lead a less useful and worthy life than he otherwise would.

Second, surplus wealth may be bequeathed at death for public purposes. This is better than the first mode but still unsatisfactory. The man who dies thus rich dies disgraced. The wealth that could have done immense good during the owner's lifetime is distributed after death by others who may not share the donor's knowledge, judgment, or intentions.

The third and proper mode — Carnegie's gospel — is that the man of wealth should consider all surplus revenues which come to him simply as trust funds, which he is called upon to administer during his lifetime for the benefit of the community. The duty of the man of wealth is to set an example of modest, unostentatious living, to provide moderately for the legitimate wants of those dependent upon him, and then to consider all surplus revenues as trust funds to be administered in the manner best calculated to produce the most beneficial results for the community.

Carnegie is emphatic that this does not mean indiscriminate charity. In bestowing charity, the main consideration should be to help those who will help themselves. Neither the individual nor the race is improved by almsgiving. The best means of benefiting the community is to place within its reach the ladders upon which the aspiring can rise — free libraries, parks, works of art, institutions of learning, hospitals, and other public institutions. The goal is not to relieve poverty directly but to create the conditions under which individuals can lift themselves through their own effort.

He argues that this doctrine reconciles the apparent conflict between the rich and the poor. Under the administration of wealth by the capable few, the surplus wealth of the few will become the property of the many in a far better form than it could have been if distributed in small sums to the people themselves. Even the poorest can be made to see this and to agree that great sums gathered by some of their fellow citizens and spent for public purposes are more valuable to them than if scattered among them in trifling amounts.

Carnegie acknowledges that this places an enormous responsibility on the wealthy individual, who must exercise the same talent and judgment in distributing his fortune as he employed in accumulating it. The man who dies rich dies disgraced — this is the creed of the gospel of wealth. The administration of wealth during one's lifetime is the final and highest duty of the man of fortune.
