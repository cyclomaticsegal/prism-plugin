# Federalist No. 10 — James Madison

> Excerpt from The Federalist Papers, No. 10, published 1787. Public domain.

Madison addresses what he considers the most dangerous vice of popular government: the violence of faction. Among the numerous advantages promised by a well-constructed Union, none deserves to be more accurately developed than its tendency to break and control this threat. Citizens in every state have complained that governments are too unstable, that the public good is disregarded in the conflicts of rival parties, and that measures are too often decided not according to the rules of justice and the rights of the minor party but by the superior force of an interested and overbearing majority.

Madison defines a faction as a number of citizens, whether amounting to a majority or minority of the whole, who are united and actuated by some common impulse of passion or of interest adverse to the rights of other citizens or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community. The problem is structural, not merely a matter of bad character. Even enlightened statesmen will not always be at the helm.

There are two methods of curing the mischiefs of faction: removing its causes, and controlling its effects. Removing the causes can be accomplished in two ways: by destroying the liberty that allows factions to form, or by giving every citizen the same opinions, passions, and interests. The first remedy is worse than the disease — liberty is essential to political life and cannot be extinguished merely because it nourishes faction. The second is impracticable. As long as human reason remains fallible and humans are free to exercise it, different opinions will form. The latent causes of faction are sown in the nature of man.

The most common and durable source of faction is the various and unequal distribution of property. Those who hold and those who are without property have ever formed distinct interests in society. Creditors and debtors, landed interests, manufacturing interests, mercantile interests, and many lesser interests grow up of necessity in civilized nations and divide them into different classes actuated by different sentiments and views. The regulation of these various and interfering interests forms the principal task of modern legislation.

Since the causes of faction cannot be removed without destroying liberty itself, relief is only to be sought in the means of controlling its effects. If a faction consists of less than a majority, the republican principle enables the majority to defeat its sinister views by regular vote. The greater danger comes from a majority faction, which can sacrifice both the public good and the rights of other citizens to its ruling passion or interest.

Madison argues that a large republic is the specific remedy. In a small democracy, a common passion or interest will be felt by a majority of the whole, and there is nothing to check the inducements to sacrifice the weaker party. In a large republic, the greater number and variety of parties and interests makes it less probable that any single faction will constitute a majority. The sphere of representation is widened, making it more likely that fit characters will be chosen. The greater variety of interests and sects within a large territory provides a security against any one faction dominating the rest.

This is Madison's decisive argument for the proposed Constitution and the extended republic it would create. A large, diverse, federated nation is not a weakness but a safeguard — the very pluralism that faction feeds upon becomes the mechanism by which faction is restrained. The Union itself is the cure for the diseases most incident to republican government.
