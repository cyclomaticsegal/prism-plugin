# On the Origin of Species — Charles Darwin

> Excerpt from On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection, published 1859. Public domain.

Darwin's argument proceeds from the familiar to the unfamiliar. He begins with variation under domestication — the well-known fact that breeders of pigeons, cattle, dogs, and crops routinely produce new varieties by selecting individuals with desirable traits and breeding from them over successive generations. The accumulated effects of selection over many generations can produce forms dramatically different from the original stock. If artificial selection wielded by human breeders can accomplish so much in a few centuries, what might natural selection accomplish over geological ages?

The mechanism of natural selection rests on several observations and inferences. First, all organisms produce far more offspring than can possibly survive. A single pair of elephants, the slowest breeders of all known animals, would produce fifteen million descendants in five hundred years if all survived and bred. Yet the number of individuals of any given species remains roughly constant over time. Therefore, there must be a severe struggle for existence among the members of every species, a struggle in which only a fraction survive to reproduce.

Second, individuals within any species vary from one another. No two members of any species are exactly alike. These variations are heritable — offspring tend to resemble their parents in the specific features by which they differ from other members of the species. Third, some of these variations will be advantageous in the struggle for existence, giving their bearers a slightly better chance of surviving and reproducing. It follows that individuals possessing favourable variations will tend to be preserved and will pass those variations to their offspring, while those with injurious variations will tend to be destroyed. This preservation of favourable variations and rejection of injurious variations Darwin calls natural selection.

The process is gradual, cumulative, and unconscious. No designing intelligence selects or directs it. Natural selection acts only by the preservation and accumulation of small inherited modifications, each profitable to the preserved being. It can act on every internal organ, on every shade of constitutional difference, on the whole machinery of life. It acts during the whole lifetime of each individual and is daily and hourly scrutinising every variation, rejecting that which is bad, preserving and adding up all that is good, silently and insensibly working whenever and wherever opportunity offers.

Darwin addresses the obvious difficulty: if species change only by the accumulation of slight successive modifications, where are the transitional forms? He argues that the geological record is imperfect in the extreme, like a history of the world in which only a few scattered pages survive. Transitional varieties would typically be less numerous than the well-adapted forms they connect, and would therefore be less likely to be preserved as fossils. The apparent gaps in the record are gaps in our knowledge, not in the continuity of nature.

The principle of divergence of character explains how natural selection produces not merely change but branching diversity. In any given area, the more diversified the descendants of any one species become in their habits and structures, the more places in the economy of nature they can seize upon. Natural selection therefore tends to favour increasing specialization and divergence, producing over time the tree-like pattern of life, with species branching from common ancestors into ever more distinct and varied forms.

Darwin concludes that there is grandeur in this view of life, with its several powers, having been originally breathed into a few forms or into one, and that whilst this planet has gone cycling on according to the fixed law of gravity, from so simple a beginning endless forms most beautiful and most wonderful have been and are being evolved.
