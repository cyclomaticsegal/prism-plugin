# An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding — David Hume

> Excerpt from An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, published 1748. Public domain.

Hume divides the objects of human reason into two kinds: relations of ideas and matters of fact. Relations of ideas include the propositions of geometry, algebra, and arithmetic — truths that are discoverable by the mere operation of thought and do not depend on what actually exists anywhere in the universe. That the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides is true whether or not any triangle exists in nature. Matters of fact, by contrast, are not ascertained in the same manner. The contrary of every matter of fact is always possible, because it can never imply a contradiction. That the sun will rise tomorrow is no less intelligible a proposition than that it will not rise, and the one cannot be demonstrated false by pure reason.

The central question then becomes: what is the foundation of all our reasoning concerning matters of fact? Hume answers that it is the relation of cause and effect. All reasonings concerning matters of fact seem to be founded on this relation. When we find that a particular object is constantly conjoined with another — that fire is followed by heat, that bread nourishes — we infer a causal connection and expect the one to follow from the other. But Hume presses further: what is the foundation of our reasoning about cause and effect itself?

He argues that causal reasoning is not founded on reason or any process of the understanding, but entirely on experience. We cannot know a priori that fire will burn or that bread will nourish. A man presented with fire for the first time could not deduce its effects from its appearance. It is only after repeated experience of the conjunction between fire and heat that we form the expectation. No amount of rational analysis of the cause alone, without experience, could ever reveal its effect.

But experience itself faces a deeper problem. When we argue from past experience to future expectations — when we infer that because fire has always burned in the past, it will burn in the future — we rely on the assumption that the future will resemble the past, that nature is uniform. Yet this assumption cannot itself be justified either by demonstrative reasoning or by experience. It cannot be demonstrated, because the contrary is conceivable. And it cannot be established by experience without circularity, since any appeal to past experience to justify the uniformity of nature already presupposes the very principle it seeks to establish.

Hume concludes that our belief in causal connections is not the product of reason but of custom or habit. After experiencing the constant conjunction of two objects — flame and heat, bread and nourishment — the mind is carried by habit to expect the one upon the appearance of the other. This is a natural instinct, not a rational inference. Custom is the great guide of human life. Without it, we would be entirely ignorant of every matter of fact beyond what is immediately present to the memory and senses.

This analysis does not lead Hume to sceptical paralysis but to a recalibration of epistemic ambition. We cannot achieve demonstrative certainty about the causal structure of the world, but we can and do navigate it successfully through the natural operations of the mind. The wise man proportions his belief to the evidence, understanding that even our strongest empirical conclusions rest ultimately on habit and probability rather than logical necessity. Hume's argument permanently altered the landscape of epistemology, compelling Kant to develop his critical philosophy as a direct response and establishing the problem of induction as one of the fundamental challenges in the theory of knowledge.
