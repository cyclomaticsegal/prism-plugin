# Principia Mathematica — Isaac Newton

> Excerpt from Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica, published 1687. Public domain.

At the opening of Book III of the Principia, Newton lays down his Rules of Reasoning in Philosophy, which establish the methodological foundation for the entire work. These rules articulate the principles by which natural philosophy should proceed in drawing conclusions from observations, and they represent one of the first systematic statements of the scientific method as practiced in modern physics.

Rule I states: We are to admit no more causes of natural things than such as are both true and sufficient to explain their appearances. Nature is pleased with simplicity and does not indulge in the luxury of superfluous causes. This is the principle of parsimony — when a simpler explanation suffices, there is no warrant for introducing additional causes. Newton does not deny that complex phenomena may have complex explanations, but he insists that the burden of proof lies with complexity. If the motion of the planets can be explained by gravitation alone, we should not invoke other forces without necessity.

Rule II follows directly: Therefore to the same natural effects we must, as far as possible, assign the same causes. The respiration of a man and of a beast, the fall of stones in Europe and in America, the light of the kitchen fire and of the sun — where the effects are the same, the causes should be presumed the same until evidence compels us to distinguish them. This rule prevents the fragmentation of natural philosophy into disconnected explanations for phenomena that share a common character.

Rule III represents a bold inductive step: The qualities of bodies which admit neither intensification nor remission of degrees, and which are found to belong to all bodies within the reach of our experiments, are to be esteemed the universal qualities of all bodies whatsoever. Since we know that all bodies within our experience are extended, hard, impenetrable, movable, and subject to inertia, we may conclude that these qualities belong to all bodies universally, including those beyond our direct observation. Newton specifically extends this principle to the gravitational force: since all bodies within reach of experiment gravitate toward the earth and toward each other, gravitation is to be attributed to all bodies universally.

Rule IV, added in later editions, addresses the status of inductive conclusions: In experimental philosophy we are to look upon propositions inferred by general induction from phenomena as accurately or very nearly true, notwithstanding any contrary hypotheses that may be imagined, till such time as other phenomena occur by which they may either be made more accurate or liable to exceptions. This rule protects the results of careful induction from premature dismissal by speculative hypotheses. A well-established empirical generalization is not to be overturned by mere theoretical objection — only by new phenomena that actually contradict it.

Taken together, the four rules constitute a methodology of constrained empiricism. Newton rejects the rationalist approach of Descartes, who built natural philosophy upon supposedly self-evident first principles. He equally rejects undisciplined speculation. The path forward is to begin with observed phenomena, infer causes by the simplest and most uniform explanations available, generalize cautiously to the universal, and hold firmly to the results until new observations compel revision.

The rules embody Newton's celebrated dictum: hypotheses non fingo — I frame no hypotheses. This does not mean that Newton had no theoretical ideas. It means that he refuses to present speculative conjectures as established truths. Whatever is not deduced from the phenomena is to be called a hypothesis, and hypotheses have no place in experimental philosophy. The function of natural philosophy is to discover the forces of nature from the phenomena of motion and then to demonstrate the other phenomena from those forces.
