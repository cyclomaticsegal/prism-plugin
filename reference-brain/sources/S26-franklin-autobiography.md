# The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin — Benjamin Franklin

> Excerpt from The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin, written 1771-1790, published posthumously 1791. Public domain.

Franklin's Autobiography is, among other things, a manual of systematic self-improvement — an account of how a tradesman's son from Boston with two years of formal schooling made himself into a scientist, diplomat, inventor, and statesman through the deliberate cultivation of practical virtues. The method he describes has become one of the most widely imitated programs of personal development in history.

At about the age of twenty-seven, Franklin conceived the bold and arduous project of arriving at moral perfection. He wished not merely to know what was right but to habituate himself to doing it. He drew up a list of thirteen virtues, each accompanied by a short precept. Temperance: eat not to dullness, drink not to elevation. Silence: speak not but what may benefit others or yourself; avoid trifling conversation. Order: let all your things have their places; let each part of your business have its time. Resolution: resolve to perform what you ought; perform without fail what you resolve. Frugality: make no expense but to do good to others or yourself; waste nothing. Industry: lose no time; be always employed in something useful; cut off all unnecessary actions. Sincerity: use no hurtful deceit; think innocently and justly, and if you speak, speak accordingly. Justice: wrong none by doing injuries or omitting the benefits that are your duty. Moderation: avoid extremes; forbear resenting injuries so much as you think they deserve. Cleanliness: tolerate no uncleanliness in body, clothes, or habitation. Tranquillity: be not disturbed at trifles or at accidents common or unavoidable. Chastity. Humility: imitate Jesus and Socrates.

His method was to focus on one virtue at a time, for one week, tracking his performance in a small book with a page for each virtue, marked with columns for the days of the week and rows for each virtue. Every evening, he reviewed the day and marked a black spot for every fault committed against the focal virtue. The goal was to keep the focal virtue's row clean for a full week before moving to the next. By cycling through all thirteen virtues in thirteen weeks, he could complete four courses in a year.

Franklin candidly acknowledges that he never achieved the perfection he aimed at. The virtue of Order gave him the most trouble — his business and social life created constant interruptions to any fixed schedule, and his natural temperament resisted the discipline of systematic arrangement. He compares himself to a man who wanted to have a perfectly bright axe but found the grinding so laborious that he decided he liked a speckled axe best. Yet he insists that the attempt, even where it fell short, was enormously valuable. He attributed his health, his fortune, his reputation, and whatever share of happiness he enjoyed to this early program.

The broader philosophy underlying the program is pragmatic and secular. Franklin does not pursue virtue because God commands it or because metaphysics requires it, but because he finds through experience that virtuous habits produce tangible benefits — better health, greater trust from others, clearer thinking, and compound success over time. He treats moral development as a skill to be acquired through practice, tracking, and iteration, much like a craft or trade.

Franklin's approach to intellectual self-improvement follows the same pattern. He taught himself to write by studying the Spectator essays, breaking them apart, reassembling them from notes, and comparing his versions to the originals. He learned arithmetic, navigation, and multiple languages through the same disciplined practice. He founded a mutual improvement society, the Junto, where tradesmen and artisans met weekly to discuss questions of morals, politics, and natural philosophy — pooling their knowledge and connections for mutual benefit.

The Autobiography thus presents life itself as a project amenable to rational management: identify objectives, develop systematic methods, track progress, learn from failures, and iterate.
