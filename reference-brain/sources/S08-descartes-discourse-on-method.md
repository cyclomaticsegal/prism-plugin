# Discourse on the Method — Rene Descartes

> Excerpt from Discourse on the Method of Rightly Conducting One's Reason and of Seeking Truth in the Sciences, published 1637. Public domain.

Descartes recounts his intellectual autobiography as a path toward the discovery of a secure foundation for knowledge. Having been educated in the best schools of Europe, he found himself dissatisfied with the entire edifice of learning he had received. Philosophy, despite centuries of cultivation by the finest minds, contained nothing that was not subject to dispute. The sciences built upon philosophy were correspondingly unstable. Mathematics alone seemed to yield certainty, yet its methods had not been applied to matters of real importance. He resolved to seek within himself, through the exercise of his own reason, what the schools could not provide.

He adopted four rules of method. First, never to accept anything as true that he did not clearly know to be so — to avoid precipitation and prejudice, and to include nothing in his judgments beyond what presented itself to his mind so clearly and distinctly that he had no occasion to doubt it. Second, to divide each difficulty into as many parts as possible in order to resolve it more easily. Third, to conduct his thoughts in an orderly fashion, beginning with the simplest and most easily known objects and ascending little by little to the knowledge of the most complex. Fourth, to make enumerations so complete and reviews so comprehensive that he could be assured of omitting nothing.

Descartes then undertakes a radical experiment in doubt. He resolves to reject as absolutely false everything in which he could imagine the least doubt, in order to see whether, after this process, anything at all would remain that was entirely certain. He finds that he can doubt the evidence of his senses, since the senses sometimes deceive. He can doubt the truths of mathematics, since an omnipotent deceiver might have arranged things so that he errs even when he seems to reason most clearly. He can doubt the existence of the external world, his own body, and everything he has ever believed.

But in the very act of doubting everything, he discovers something that cannot be doubted. The fact that he is thinking — even if thinking erroneously, even if being deceived — is itself indubitable. For if he doubts, he thinks; and if he thinks, he must exist as a thinking thing. This is the celebrated cogito ergo sum: I think, therefore I am. It survives every possible doubt and serves as the first principle of the philosophy Descartes is seeking.

From this foundation, Descartes proceeds to establish further certainties. He examines the nature of this thinking self and concludes that it is a substance whose whole essence consists in thinking, and which requires no place or material thing for its existence. The mind is entirely distinct from the body. He then considers why the cogito is so certain and arrives at a general rule: the things we conceive very clearly and very distinctly are all true. But this rule itself requires a guarantee, which Descartes finds in the existence and non-deceptive nature of God.

The Discourse thus establishes a method of systematic doubt leading to indubitable first principles, from which further knowledge can be deduced with mathematical certainty. It inaugurates modern rationalism and the philosophical turn toward subjectivity — the idea that the foundation of knowledge lies not in tradition, authority, or sensory experience, but in the self-transparent activity of the thinking subject. The separation of mind and body that Descartes proposes becomes one of the defining problems of subsequent philosophy, shaping debates about consciousness, free will, and the relationship between mental and physical reality.
