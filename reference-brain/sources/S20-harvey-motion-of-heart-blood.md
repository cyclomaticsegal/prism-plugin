# On the Motion of the Heart and Blood — William Harvey

> Excerpt from Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus, published 1628. Public domain.

Harvey's treatise overturns the Galenic theory of blood that had prevailed for nearly fifteen hundred years. Galen had taught that blood was continuously manufactured in the liver from food, distributed through the veins to nourish the body, and consumed in the tissues. A small amount of blood supposedly seeped through invisible pores in the septum dividing the heart's ventricles, where it mixed with air from the lungs to produce vital spirits distributed through the arteries. There was no concept of circulation — blood was thought to ebb and flow like a tide, created and consumed in a linear process.

Harvey begins by describing his careful observations of the heart's action in living animals. He notes that when the heart contracts, it becomes paler and harder, and blood is expelled from the ventricles into the great arteries. When it relaxes, it becomes softer and distended as blood flows in from the veins. The motion of the heart consists of a systole — a muscular contraction that expels blood — and a diastole — a relaxation during which the chambers refill. This was itself a correction to prevailing teaching, which held that the heart's active phase was diastole, during which it supposedly sucked blood inward.

Harvey then demonstrates that the blood moves in one direction only through the heart. The valves of the heart, whose structure he describes in detail, prevent backflow. Blood enters the right ventricle from the veins, is pumped to the lungs through the pulmonary artery, returns from the lungs through the pulmonary vein to the left ventricle, and is then driven into the aorta and distributed to the body through the arterial system. The septum between the ventricles is solid — Harvey could find no pores or passages through it, despite the most diligent searching.

The decisive argument comes from quantitative reasoning. Harvey estimates the capacity of the left ventricle at roughly two ounces of blood. The heart beats roughly seventy-two times per minute. In one hour, the heart therefore pumps approximately 540 pounds of blood — a quantity vastly exceeding the total amount of blood in the body and utterly impossible to explain as newly manufactured from food. The blood must therefore be going around in a circle, returning to the heart through the veins after being distributed through the arteries.

Harvey confirms this with a series of ligature experiments. When a tight ligature is applied to the arm, cutting off both arterial and venous flow, the arm below the ligature becomes pale and cold, while the veins do not swell. When the ligature is loosened to permit arterial flow while still obstructing venous return, the veins below the ligature swell and the arm becomes engorged. This demonstrates that blood flows outward through the arteries and returns through the veins.

He further points to the valves in the veins, whose existence was already known but whose function had not been understood. These valves allow blood to flow only toward the heart, preventing backflow. Harvey demonstrates this by pressing blood out of a segment of vein and showing that it refills only from the direction away from the heart, not from the cardiac side.

Harvey acknowledges that he cannot directly observe the connection between the smallest arteries and the smallest veins — the capillaries would not be seen until Malpighi's microscopic observations decades later. But he infers their existence from the necessity of his argument: the blood must pass from the arteries to the veins through the tissues, even though the passages are too small to see.

The treatise exemplifies a new method in biological science: combining direct anatomical observation with quantitative reasoning and experimental testing to overturn ancient authority. Harvey does not merely assert; he demonstrates, calculating, ligating, observing, and inviting his readers to repeat the experiments themselves.
