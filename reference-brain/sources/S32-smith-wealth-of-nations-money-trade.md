# The Wealth of Nations — Adam Smith

> Excerpt from An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations, Book IV, published 1776. Public domain.

Smith devotes a substantial portion of the Wealth of Nations to dismantling the mercantile system — the prevailing doctrine that identified national wealth with the accumulation of gold and silver and that sought to maintain a favourable balance of trade through tariffs, bounties, and prohibitions. This critique is inseparable from Smith's positive account of money and its proper function in a commercial society.

Money, Smith argues, is not wealth itself but merely the instrument of commerce. Gold and silver are commodities like any other, useful for certain purposes but not to be confused with the real wealth of a nation, which consists of the annual produce of its land and labour — the consumable goods that actually sustain and improve human life. The mercantilists committed the fundamental error of confusing the sign of wealth with the thing itself. A country may have vast quantities of gold and yet be poor if its productive powers are weak; another may have little gold and yet be rich in the goods that matter.

The origin of money lies in the inconveniences of barter. In a society without a common medium of exchange, every transaction requires a double coincidence of wants — the butcher must find not merely someone who wants meat but someone who wants meat and has something the butcher needs. This difficulty naturally leads to the adoption of some universally accepted commodity as a medium of exchange. Various societies have used cattle, salt, shells, tobacco, dried cod, and nails for this purpose. Metals proved most suitable because of their durability, divisibility, and uniformity, and gold and silver eventually prevailed as the metals most widely accepted.

But the function of money is purely instrumental. It is the great wheel of circulation, the great instrument of commerce. The revenue of a society consists not in the gold and silver pieces but in the goods those pieces purchase. Increasing the quantity of gold in a country does not increase its real wealth any more than multiplying the number of tickets at a theatre increases the quantity of entertainment.

Smith extends this analysis to international trade. The mercantile system sought to ensure that a nation exported more than it imported, so that the balance would be paid in gold. Smith argues that this policy is based on a double misunderstanding: it overvalues gold and it misunderstands the nature of trade. Trade is not a zero-sum competition in which one nation's gain is another's loss. When two nations trade freely, both benefit — each exports what it can produce cheaply and imports what would be costly to produce at home. The natural advantages of soil, climate, and situation mean that some countries can produce wine more cheaply, others cloth, others grain. By allowing each to specialise according to its advantages, free trade maximises the total produce available to all.

Restrictions on trade — tariffs, prohibitions, and monopoly privileges — benefit particular domestic producers at the expense of the general public. The monopoly of the home market gives the favoured industry an artificial advantage, directing capital into channels where it would not naturally flow. But the annual revenue of every society is always precisely equal to the exchangeable value of the whole annual produce of its industry. By diverting capital from its natural course, trade restrictions diminish rather than increase the total value of that produce.

Smith acknowledges certain exceptions to the principle of free trade. Defence is of much more importance than opulence, and he concedes that the Navigation Acts, which reserved British colonial trade for British ships, were justified on grounds of national security. He also allows for retaliatory tariffs in certain diplomatic circumstances and for the gradual rather than sudden removal of protections that have long been in place, to avoid sudden dislocation.

The deeper argument is that the wealth of nations grows not through the hoarding of precious metals or the manipulation of trade balances but through the productive employment of labour and capital in those channels where they naturally find the greatest returns. The invisible hand of individual self-interest, operating through free markets, allocates resources more efficiently than any system of government direction.
