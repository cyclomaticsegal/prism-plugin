# A Vindication of the Rights of Woman — Mary Wollstonecraft

> Excerpt from A Vindication of the Rights of Woman: with Strictures on Political and Moral Subjects, published 1792. Public domain.

Wollstonecraft's central argument is that women are not naturally inferior to men but appear so only because they are denied the education that would develop their rational faculties. The prevailing system of female education, designed to produce pleasing and obedient companions rather than thinking human beings, creates the very weakness it then cites as evidence of natural incapacity. Women are taught from childhood to cultivate beauty, docility, and the arts of pleasing men. Their minds are neglected while their bodies and manners are polished. The result is beings who are cunning rather than wise, manipulative rather than principled, dependent rather than self-sufficient — and then society points to these traits as proof that women are fit for nothing better.

Wollstonecraft directs her criticism most forcefully at Rousseau, who in Emile had argued that the education of women should be designed entirely in relation to men. Women, Rousseau contended, should be trained to please, to be useful, to make themselves loved and honoured, to educate the young, to care for the grown, to counsel, to console, and to make life agreeable and sweet. Wollstonecraft finds this program not merely unjust but self-defeating. A woman whose entire education consists of learning to attract and retain a husband has no resources when her beauty fades, no foundation for genuine companionship, and no capacity to educate her own children except in the same superficial accomplishments.

If women are rational beings — and Wollstonecraft insists that this is either true or it is not, and if not, then the pretensions of all mankind to rationality are equally suspect — then they are entitled to the same education that develops rationality in men. She does not argue that women and men are identical in all respects, but that both sexes share the defining human capacity for reason and virtue, and that both are degraded when this capacity is deliberately stunted.

Wollstonecraft extends the argument to the political sphere. She draws a direct parallel between the subordination of women and the subordination of subjects under tyrannical government. In both cases, an arbitrary authority prevents the governed from developing the faculties that would enable self-governance. The tyrant and the husband who demands blind obedience employ the same logic: the subject is unfit for freedom, and therefore must remain in subjection. But the unfitness is the product of the subjection, not its justification.

The reform Wollstonecraft proposes is fundamentally educational. Girls should be educated alongside boys in public schools, studying the same subjects, developing the same habits of rigorous thought, and learning to value themselves as rational agents rather than decorative objects. This would produce women capable of genuine friendship with their husbands rather than mere dependence, women who could manage households and raise children with intelligence and principle, and women who could contribute to public life as informed citizens.

Wollstonecraft argues that the current system harms men as well as women. Men who are accustomed to the society of uneducated women become themselves superficial, valuing appearance over substance. The domestic sphere, debased by the ignorance and frivolity of its female inhabitants, fails to provide the moral education that children need. The entire society suffers when half its members are kept in artificial ignorance. Strengthening the female mind by enlarging it would end the blind obedience demanded by tyranny in every form, and reform would ripple outward from the household to the state.
