# Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals — Immanuel Kant

> Excerpt from Grundlegung zur Metaphysik der Sitten, published 1785. Public domain.

Kant seeks to identify and establish the supreme principle of morality — a principle that derives not from experience, custom, or the consequences of action, but from pure practical reason alone. He begins with the claim that the only thing that is good without qualification is a good will. Intelligence, wit, judgment, courage, resolution, and perseverance are undoubtedly good in many respects, but they can also become extremely bad and harmful if the will that makes use of them is not good. Even the gifts of fortune — power, riches, honour, health, and general well-being — can produce arrogance and presumption if not corrected by a good will.

A good will is good not because of what it accomplishes but because of what it wills — that is, because of its maxim or principle of action. Its value lies entirely in the principle upon which it acts, not in the results it achieves. Even if by some particular disfavour of fortune or by the niggardly provision of a stepmotherly nature, this will should wholly lack the power to accomplish its purpose, it would still shine by its own light as a thing which has its full worth in itself.

The principle that governs the good will is duty. Kant distinguishes between actions done merely in conformity with duty and actions done from duty. A shopkeeper who deals honestly because dishonesty would harm his business acts in conformity with duty but not from duty — his motive is self-interest, not moral principle. Only actions performed from the motive of duty have genuine moral worth. The moral worth of an action does not lie in the effect expected from it, but solely in the principle of the will by which the action is determined.

Kant formulates the supreme moral principle as the categorical imperative, which he expresses in several formulations. The first and most famous is the formula of universal law: Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law. When contemplating an action, ask whether the principle on which you propose to act could consistently be willed as a law governing everyone. If not, the action is morally impermissible.

Kant illustrates with examples. A man in financial distress considers making a promise he knows he cannot keep. Could he will that everyone make false promises when convenient? No — for if lying promises were universal, the institution of promising would collapse and no one would believe any promise, making the very act self-defeating. A maxim of false promising cannot be universalized without contradiction and is therefore morally wrong.

The second major formulation is the formula of humanity: Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end. Rational beings have dignity, not merely price. They are not tools to be used but autonomous agents whose purposes deserve respect. To deceive, coerce, or manipulate another person is to treat them merely as a means — to subordinate their rational agency to your own purposes.

The third formulation, the formula of the kingdom of ends, envisions a community of rational beings legislating universal moral laws for themselves. Each rational agent is both subject to and author of the moral law. The moral community is one in which every member treats every other member as an end and in which the laws governing conduct are those that all could rationally endorse.

Kant's ethics is thus formal rather than material. It does not prescribe specific actions or pursue particular outcomes. It establishes a procedure — the test of universalizability — by which any proposed action can be evaluated. The moral law binds not because it produces good consequences but because it is the law that rational beings give to themselves through the exercise of their own practical reason.
