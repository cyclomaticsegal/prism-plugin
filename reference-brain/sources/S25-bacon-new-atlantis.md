# New Atlantis — Francis Bacon

> Excerpt from New Atlantis, published 1627. Public domain.

Bacon's utopian fiction describes the discovery of Bensalem, a hidden island civilization in the Pacific whose defining institution is Salomon's House — a state-sponsored research establishment dedicated to the systematic investigation of nature and the application of knowledge to the improvement of human life. The narrative is presented through the eyes of European sailors who stumble upon the island and are astonished by its technological sophistication and social order.

Salomon's House, also called the College of the Six Days' Works, is described by one of its Fathers in a speech that constitutes the philosophical heart of the work. The end of our foundation is the knowledge of causes and the secret motions of things, and the enlarging of the bounds of human empire, to the effecting of all things possible. This dual purpose — understanding nature and extending human power over it — defines the Baconian vision of science as simultaneously contemplative and practical.

The institution possesses resources that would be extraordinary even by modern standards. It maintains deep caves for experiments conducted underground, including the production of new metals and the study of prolongation of life. It has tall towers for astronomical and meteorological observation. It operates great lakes for experiments on fish and birds, and enclosed parks and gardens where all manner of beasts, birds, and plants are kept for study, breeding, and experimentation. The researchers of Salomon's House have developed means of making new species, grafting and altering the growth of plants, and modifying the characteristics of animals — a vision of biotechnology centuries ahead of its realization.

The mechanical arts are equally advanced. The institution has engine-houses where new machines and instruments of motion are developed. It produces artificial imitations of natural phenomena, including artificial snow, hail, rain, and thunder. It has optical houses with devices for magnification, multiplication, and projection of light. It has sound-houses where musical instruments and techniques for transmitting sound over distance are developed. It has perfume-houses, dispensatories for medicines, and mathematical houses for instruments of geometry and astronomy.

The organization of Salomon's House is as deliberate as its equipment. Its members are divided into specialized groups. Merchants of Light travel abroad in disguise to gather knowledge from other nations. Depredators collect experiments from books and published works. Mystery-Men compile the experiments of mechanical arts and liberal sciences. Pioneers try new experiments of their own devising. Compilers draw the experiments into tables and observations that yield axioms and aphorisms. Benefactors examine these results and identify practical applications. Lamps direct new experiments based on what has been learned. Inoculators execute those experiments. Interpreters of Nature raise the discoveries into greater observations, axioms, and aphorisms.

This elaborate division of intellectual labour mirrors the division of manual labour that Adam Smith would later describe as the engine of economic productivity. Bacon applies the principle of specialization to the production of knowledge itself, anticipating the structure of modern research institutions with their principal investigators, laboratory technicians, theoreticians, and applied scientists.

The political implications are significant. Bensalem prospers not through military conquest or commercial exploitation but through the systematic cultivation of knowledge. The wealth and power of the state derive from its scientific establishment. The Fathers of Salomon's House are the most respected figures in the land, honored above all others. Bacon's utopia places the scientist, not the priest, the warrior, or the merchant, at the summit of the social order — a radical reimagining of the relationship between knowledge and power that would profoundly influence the founding of the Royal Society and the entire subsequent trajectory of Western science.
