# Self-Reliance — Ralph Waldo Emerson

> Excerpt from Self-Reliance, published 1841. Public domain.

Emerson's essay is a manifesto for individual integrity against the deadening force of social conformity. Trust thyself: every heart vibrates to that iron string. The great men of history have distinguished themselves not by unusual talent but by the courage to express their own thought without apology or qualification. In every work of genius, we recognize our own rejected thoughts; they come back to us with a certain alienated majesty. The lesson is clear: we must learn to detect and watch that gleam of light which flashes across the mind from within, more than the lustre of the firmament of bards and sages.

Society everywhere is in conspiracy against the manhood of every one of its members. It demands conformity and punishes originality. The virtue in most request is conformity. Self-reliance is its aversion. Whoso would be a man must be a nonconformist. He who would gather immortal palms must not be hindered by the name of goodness but must explore if it be goodness. Nothing is at last sacred but the integrity of your own mind.

Emerson warns against the foolish consistency that constrains thought and action. A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds, adored by little statesmen and philosophers and divines. With consistency, a great soul has simply nothing to do. The fear of contradicting what one has previously said or done traps men in positions they have outgrown. Speak what you think now in hard words, and tomorrow speak what tomorrow thinks in hard words again, though it contradict everything you said today. To be misunderstood is not a failure. Pythagoras was misunderstood, and Socrates, and Jesus, and Luther, and Copernicus, and Galileo, and Newton.

The pressure to conform operates through institutions, opinions, and the judgment of others. Men measure their worth by what others think of them rather than by what they know of themselves. They travel abroad to learn from the ruins of other civilizations while ignoring the revelation available in their own experience. They join movements and subscribe to creeds not because they have thought these things through but because doing so is easier than standing alone. Prayer itself, when it is petitionary — asking for a private good — is a form of conformity to the assumption that external forces must supply what the self should provide.

Emerson grounds self-reliance not in mere stubbornness but in a metaphysical conviction. There is a deeper self beneath the social self, a universal soul that speaks through each individual when that individual has the courage to listen. The soul is not a private possession but a channel through which the divine communicates with the world. To trust oneself is to trust this deeper current. It is not egoism but fidelity to the universal principle that expresses itself uniquely through each person.

The practical implications are sweeping. Do your work, and you shall reinforce yourself. A man must consider what a blindman's-buff is this game of conformity. Every decent and well-spoken individual affects and sways me more than is right. Emerson calls for economic self-sufficiency as a foundation for intellectual independence. A man who depends on others for his livelihood will inevitably defer to their opinions. The scholar, the artist, the thinker must secure a material basis that allows them to speak the truth without fear of consequence.

The essay culminates in a vision of a society transformed by self-reliant individuals. When each person follows the dictates of their own nature rather than the prescriptions of convention, a new creative energy enters the world. Insist on yourself; never imitate. Nothing can bring you peace but yourself. Nothing can bring you peace but the triumph of principles.
