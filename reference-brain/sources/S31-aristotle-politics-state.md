# Politics — Aristotle

> Excerpt from Politics, Book I (On the Nature of the State), circa 350 BCE. Public domain.

Aristotle opens the Politics with the assertion that the state is a creation of nature and that man is by nature a political animal. Every state is a community of some kind, and every community is established with a view to some good; for men always act in order to obtain that which they think good. The state or political community, which is the highest of all communities and which embraces all the rest, aims at good in a greater degree than any other and at the highest good.

To understand the state, Aristotle proceeds by his characteristic method of analysis: breaking the complex whole into its simple parts. The state is composed of households, and each household is composed of the elementary relations that make up domestic life — the relation of master and slave, husband and wife, parent and child. By examining these primary associations and tracing how they combine into villages and finally into states, we can understand the nature and purpose of political community.

The household exists for the satisfaction of daily needs. When several households unite for something more than the supply of daily needs, the village comes into being. When several villages are united in a single complete community, large enough to be nearly or quite self-sufficing, the state comes into existence, originating in the bare needs of life and continuing in existence for the sake of a good life. The state is not merely an alliance for mutual defence or a compact for exchange and trade. It exists for the purpose of enabling its citizens to live well — to exercise the virtues and achieve the good life that is the natural end of human existence.

This teleological argument — that the state exists by nature because it fulfils the natural end of human beings — leads Aristotle to his famous claim about human nature. He who is unable to live in society, or who has no need because he is sufficient for himself, must be either a beast or a god; he is no part of a state. The proof that the state is a creation of nature and prior to the individual is that the individual, when isolated, is not self-sufficing; and therefore he is like a part in relation to the whole. The man who first founded the state was the greatest of benefactors, for man, when perfected, is the best of animals, but when separated from law and justice, he is the worst of all.

Aristotle devotes considerable attention to the institution of slavery, which he attempts to justify on natural grounds. Some men are slaves by nature — those who are deficient in rational self-direction and are better off governed by others, just as the body is naturally ruled by the soul and the animal by the human. The natural slave benefits from the relationship because he participates in reason sufficiently to apprehend it but not to possess it independently. This argument, however strained and morally repugnant to modern readers, reveals Aristotle's method of grounding political institutions in claims about nature and natural hierarchy.

On property, Aristotle defends private ownership as more productive and more conducive to virtue than communal arrangements. When everyone owns everything, no one takes care of anything. Private property provides incentives for diligent management and creates the possibility of generosity — one cannot be liberal with what is not one's own. But Aristotle also insists on moderation. The acquisition of wealth has a natural limit set by the needs of the household and the good life. The unlimited pursuit of money for its own sake — chrematistics — is unnatural and corrupting, because it mistakes the means of life for its end.

The Politics thus establishes the state as a natural institution oriented toward the highest human good, distinguishes political association from mere economic or military alliance, and argues that the quality of political life depends on the character of its citizens and the justice of its constitutional arrangements.
