# Capital, Volume I — Karl Marx

> Excerpt from Das Kapital: Kritik der politischen Oekonomie, Erster Band, published 1867. Public domain.

Marx begins Capital with an analysis of the commodity, which he identifies as the elementary form of wealth in capitalist societies. The wealth of those societies presents itself as an immense accumulation of commodities, and the individual commodity as its elementary form. Understanding the commodity is therefore the necessary starting point for understanding the capitalist system as a whole.

Every commodity has a dual character. It possesses use-value — the capacity to satisfy a human want of some sort, whether physical or arising from fancy. A coat provides warmth, bread provides nourishment. Use-values are qualitatively different from one another and constitute the substance of all wealth regardless of social form. But in capitalist exchange, commodities also possess exchange-value — the quantitative proportion in which use-values of one sort are exchanged for those of another. A quarter of wheat may exchange for a given quantity of iron, silk, or gold. Exchange-value appears at first as something accidental and relative, but Marx argues that behind the fluctuating ratios of exchange lies a common substance that makes comparison possible.

This common substance is human labour in the abstract. When we abstract from the specific useful character of the labour embodied in different commodities — the tailoring in the coat, the weaving in the linen — what remains is a residue of identical, undifferentiated human labour, the expenditure of human brains, muscles, and nerves without regard to the particular form of its expenditure. The magnitude of value is determined by the quantity of socially necessary labour-time required for production — the labour-time needed under normal conditions of production, with the average degree of skill and intensity prevalent at the time.

Marx draws a critical distinction between concrete labour and abstract labour. Concrete labour produces use-values and differs qualitatively across occupations. The labour of the weaver produces cloth; the labour of the tailor produces coats. Abstract labour, by contrast, is the common element that creates value and is measured purely by duration. This dual character of labour corresponds to the dual character of the commodity itself.

The commodity form conceals the social relations that underlie it. When producers bring their goods to market, the social character of their labour appears not as a direct social relation between persons but as a relation between things. Marx calls this commodity fetishism. The products of human labour take on a mysterious character the moment they assume the commodity form, appearing to possess inherent value as a natural property rather than as a social relation. Just as in religion the products of the human brain appear as independent beings endowed with life, so in the world of commodities the products of human hands appear endowed with a life of their own.

The analysis of the value-form proceeds from simple exchange between two commodities to the money-form. In simple exchange, one commodity expresses its value in the body of another. As exchange becomes universal, one commodity is gradually singled out to serve as the universal equivalent — the form in which all other commodities express their value. This universal equivalent is money. Gold or silver does not possess value naturally; it acquires the money-form through a historical social process.

Marx's analysis of the commodity is not merely descriptive but critical. It exposes the way in which capitalist social relations appear to their participants as natural, objective, and beyond human control, when in fact they are historically specific and subject to transformation. The fetish character of commodities obscures exploitation by making it appear that value arises from things rather than from the labour of human beings working under particular social conditions.
