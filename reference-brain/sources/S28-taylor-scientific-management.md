# The Principles of Scientific Management — Frederick Winslow Taylor

> Excerpt from The Principles of Scientific Management, published 1911. Public domain.

Taylor opens with a sweeping claim: the principal object of management should be to secure the maximum prosperity for the employer, coupled with the maximum prosperity for each employee. These two aims are not in conflict but are mutually dependent. The greatest prosperity can exist only as the result of the greatest possible productivity of the men and machines of the establishment. Maximum prosperity for the employer means not merely large dividends but the development of every branch of the business to its highest state of excellence. Maximum prosperity for the employee means not merely higher wages but the development of each man to his state of maximum efficiency, so that he may be doing work of the highest quality for which his natural abilities fit him.

The obstacle to this mutual prosperity is what Taylor calls soldiering — the deliberate restriction of output by workers who believe, wrongly, that greater productivity per worker would result in fewer workers being needed. Taylor identifies two types of soldiering: natural soldiering, arising from the natural instinct and tendency of men to take it easy; and systematic soldiering, arising from workers' reasoned analysis that producing more will lead to rate cuts, layoffs, or the displacement of slower colleagues. The second form is far more damaging, because it is organized and deliberate.

The remedy is the replacement of rule-of-thumb methods with scientific management. Under the old system, workmen used whatever methods they had learned through tradition and personal experience. There was no standardization. The best methods were known, if at all, only to individual craftsmen and were not systematically studied, recorded, or taught. Under scientific management, every element of each man's work is studied by management, reduced to laws and rules, and formalized into a system. The science of doing each particular task replaces the individual judgment of the workman.

Taylor's method proceeds through four principles. First, the development of a true science for each element of a man's work, replacing the old rule-of-thumb. This means time studies, motion analysis, and the identification of the one best way to perform each operation. Second, the scientific selection and progressive development of the workman. Under the old system, workers chose their own occupations and trained themselves. Under scientific management, workers are carefully selected for their aptitudes and systematically trained to reach their highest capacity. Third, the bringing together of the science and the scientifically selected and trained workman. Management must ensure that work is actually done according to the principles developed. Fourth, an almost equal division of the work and the responsibility between management and the workmen. Under the old system, almost all of the work and the greater part of the responsibility were thrown upon the workmen. Under scientific management, management takes over all work for which it is better fitted than the workmen.

Taylor illustrates his principles with detailed case studies. His most famous example involves the handling of pig iron at Bethlehem Steel. By studying the mechanics of loading, identifying the optimal weight per load, specifying rest intervals based on fatigue analysis, and selecting a worker of the right physical type, Taylor increased output from twelve and a half tons per man per day to forty-seven and a half tons — nearly quadrupling productivity while increasing the worker's daily pay by sixty percent.

The deeper implication of Taylor's system is a fundamental transfer of knowledge and control from the shop floor to the office — from the skilled craftsman who carried the methods in his head to the management engineer who studies, codifies, and imposes them. This shift remains one of the most consequential and contested developments in the history of labour and industrial organization.
