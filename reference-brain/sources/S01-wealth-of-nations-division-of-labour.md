# The Wealth of Nations — Adam Smith

> Excerpt from An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations, published 1776. Public domain.

The greatest improvement in the productive powers of labour, and the greater part of the skill, dexterity, and judgment with which it is anywhere directed or applied, seem to have been the effects of the division of labour. Smith opens his treatise with this foundational observation, arguing that specialization is the primary engine of economic growth and national prosperity.

To illustrate the principle, Smith offers his famous example of the pin factory. A single workman, unacquainted with the trade and unfamiliar with the machinery, could scarcely produce one pin in a day. Yet when the work is divided into distinct operations — one man draws out the wire, another straightens it, a third cuts it, a fourth points it, a fifth grinds the top for receiving the head — ten persons working together can produce upwards of forty-eight thousand pins in a day. Each person, performing only one or two operations, becomes extraordinarily proficient at that narrow task. The resulting multiplication of output is not additive but geometric.

Smith identifies three distinct advantages arising from the division of labour. First, the improvement of dexterity in every particular workman. When a man's whole life is spent performing a small number of operations, he naturally achieves a degree of skill that would be impossible if his attention were dispersed across many tasks. Second, the saving of time that is commonly lost in passing from one species of work to another. A country weaver who also cultivates a small farm loses considerable time in transitioning between occupations, changing tools, and mentally reorienting. Third, the invention of machines that facilitate and abridge labour. When a man's whole attention is directed to a single object, he is far more likely to discover easier and readier methods of attaining it. Many of the machines used in manufactures were originally the inventions of common workmen who, being each employed in some very simple operation, naturally turned their thoughts toward finding easier methods of performing it.

The division of labour is not, however, the result of any deliberate human wisdom foreseeing its advantages. It is rather the necessary consequence of a natural propensity in human nature: the propensity to truck, barter, and exchange one thing for another. Man has almost constant occasion for the help of his brethren, and it is in vain for him to expect this from their benevolence only. It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest. We address ourselves not to their humanity but to their self-love, and never talk to them of our own necessities but of their advantages.

This propensity to exchange gives occasion to the division of labour. As the capacity to exchange grows — through improvements in transport, communication, and the extent of the market — the division of labour deepens. A porter, for example, differs less from a philosopher in natural talents than a mastiff does from a greyhound. But the difference in their occupations creates, over time, a vast gulf of specialized knowledge and capability.

Smith warns that the extent of the division of labour is limited by the extent of the market. When the market is very small, no person can have any encouragement to dedicate himself entirely to one employment, for want of the power to exchange the surplus of his own labour for such parts of the produce of other men's labour as he has occasion for. Water carriage opens wider markets than land transport, which is why civilizations along coastlines and navigable rivers develop faster.

The argument establishes the foundation for Smith's broader system: that wealth is not gold or silver but the annual produce of a nation's land and labour, and that this produce is maximized not by central direction but by the self-organizing power of exchange among freely specializing individuals.
