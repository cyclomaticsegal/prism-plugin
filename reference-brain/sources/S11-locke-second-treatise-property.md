# Second Treatise of Government — John Locke

> Excerpt from Two Treatises of Government, published 1689. Public domain.

Locke's chapter on property in the Second Treatise establishes one of the most influential accounts of how individuals come to own things in a world originally given to mankind in common. God gave the world to men in common, Locke argues, but He also gave them reason to make use of it to the best advantage of life and convenience. The earth and all its fruits belong naturally to all mankind. The question is how any individual can come to have a private property in anything, when the whole of creation is a shared inheritance.

Locke's answer begins with the one thing every person indisputably owns: his own person. Every man has a property in his own person, and this nobody has any right to but himself. The labour of his body and the work of his hands are properly his. When a man removes something from the state in which nature has placed it and mixes his labour with it, he thereby makes it his property. He that gathers acorns from beneath an oak or apples from wild trees has thereby appropriated them to himself. Nobody can deny that the nourishment is his. The labour of gathering put a distinction between them and the common, and added something to them more than what nature had done, thereby creating a private right.

This labour theory of property comes with an important qualification that Locke calls the sufficiency proviso: the appropriation of previously unowned resources is legitimate only when there is enough and as good left in common for others. A man may legitimately enclose land and call it his own so long as the remaining commons is sufficient for everyone else's needs. In Locke's view, this condition was easily met in a world with vast uncultivated territories. In the beginning, all the world was America — abundant, largely unworked, and open to appropriation by anyone willing to labour upon it.

A second limitation is the spoilage proviso. No man may appropriate more than he can make use of before it spoils. God has given us all things richly to enjoy, but not to waste. If a man gathers more fruit than he can eat, and it rots in his possession, he has offended against the law of nature. He has taken more than his share and robbed others of what they might have used.

However, Locke argues that the invention of money effectively dissolves the spoilage constraint. When men agree to place value on durable substances like gold and silver, it becomes possible to accumulate wealth without spoilage. Money does not rot. A man may sell his surplus fruit for gold and hold the gold indefinitely without violating the natural law against waste. This tacit consent to the use of money thus permits, and even encourages, the accumulation of unequal property holdings.

Locke further argues that private property and the labour that produces it benefit everyone, not just the proprietor. An acre of enclosed, cultivated land produces ten times the provisions of an acre left in common. The industrious and rational improve the earth and thereby increase the total stock available to mankind. Even those who own no land are better provisioned in a society of private property and agriculture than they would be in a primitive commons.

The political consequence is direct: the preservation of property is the chief end for which men enter into civil society. Government exists to protect the natural rights to life, liberty, and estate. Any government that systematically violates these rights loses its legitimacy and may be justly resisted.
