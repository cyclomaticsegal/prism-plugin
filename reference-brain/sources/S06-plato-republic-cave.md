# The Republic — Plato

> Excerpt from The Republic, Book VII (Allegory of the Cave), circa 380 BCE. Public domain.

Socrates asks Glaucon to imagine a group of human beings who have lived their entire lives chained inside an underground cave, facing a blank wall. Behind them burns a fire, and between the fire and the prisoners runs a low wall along which people carry figures of men, animals, and objects, whose shadows are cast onto the wall the prisoners face. The prisoners, knowing nothing else, take these shadows for the whole of reality. They give names to the shadows, observe their patterns, and develop a kind of expertise in predicting which shadow will appear next. Among the prisoners, those most skilled at this shadow-knowledge are honoured as the wisest.

Now suppose one prisoner is freed from his chains and compelled to turn around. He sees the fire and the objects that cast the shadows, and is bewildered. His eyes, accustomed to darkness, are pained by the light. He is told that what he previously took for reality was mere illusion, and that these objects are more real than the shadows. He does not believe it. The shadows seem sharper, more familiar, more convincingly real than these strange new things.

If this prisoner is then dragged upward out of the cave into the sunlight, the pain intensifies. He is blinded by the brightness and can see nothing at all. Only gradually does his vision adjust — first to shadows in the world above, then to reflections in water, then to the objects themselves, and finally to the sun, which he comes to understand as the source of light and the cause of everything visible. He realizes that the sun governs the seasons, the years, and all that is seen in the world, and that it is in a manner the cause of all those things he and his fellow prisoners had been seeing as shadows.

Socrates draws the philosophical parallel explicitly. The cave represents the world of appearances — the visible, sensory realm in which most people live. The shadows are the ordinary objects of sense perception, which most take to be the ultimate reality. The ascent out of the cave represents the soul's journey toward true knowledge, from opinion based on appearances to understanding based on reason. The sun represents the Form of the Good, the highest object of knowledge, which illuminates all other truths and makes genuine understanding possible.

The freed prisoner who returns to the cave faces ridicule and hostility. His eyes, now accustomed to the light, cannot see well in the darkness. He performs poorly at the shadow-guessing games. The other prisoners conclude that his journey has ruined his sight and that it is not worth even attempting the ascent. If anyone tried to free them and lead them upward, they would kill him if they could — an unmistakable allusion to the trial and execution of Socrates.

The allegory serves multiple purposes in Plato's argument. It illustrates the distinction between the visible world and the intelligible world, between opinion and knowledge, between becoming and being. It grounds Plato's argument that philosophers — those who have made the ascent and seen the Good — are the only ones fit to rule, precisely because they understand both realms and are not deceived by appearances. And it dramatizes the painful, disorienting nature of genuine education, which is not the filling of an empty vessel but the turning of the whole soul from darkness toward light.
