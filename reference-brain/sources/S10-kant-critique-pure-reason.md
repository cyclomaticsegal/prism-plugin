# Critique of Pure Reason — Immanuel Kant

> Excerpt from Critik der reinen Vernunft, published 1781. Public domain.

Kant frames the central problem of the Critique with a single question: How are synthetic a priori judgments possible? To understand why this question matters, one must grasp the distinctions it contains. Analytic judgments are those in which the predicate is already contained in the concept of the subject — "all bachelors are unmarried" is true by definition and tells us nothing new about the world. Synthetic judgments, by contrast, add something to the concept of the subject that was not already contained in it — "all bodies are heavy" connects two concepts that are not logically bound together. A priori judgments are known independently of experience. A posteriori judgments depend on experience for their justification.

The empiricists, especially Hume, had argued that all substantive knowledge is a posteriori — derived from and justified by experience. Analytic truths are knowable a priori but tell us nothing about reality. The rationalists had claimed that pure reason alone could yield knowledge of God, the soul, and the fundamental nature of reality. Kant's revolutionary move is to argue that there exists a class of judgments that are both synthetic and a priori — judgments that genuinely extend our knowledge yet are known independently of particular experiences. The truths of mathematics and the fundamental principles of natural science, such as the principle that every event has a cause, belong to this class.

How is this possible? Kant's answer constitutes his Copernican revolution in philosophy. Rather than assuming that our knowledge must conform to objects, he proposes that objects must conform to our knowledge — or more precisely, to the conditions under which we can experience objects. The mind does not passively receive an imprint from the external world. Instead, it actively structures experience according to its own inherent forms.

These forms operate at two levels. At the level of sensibility, space and time are the pure forms of intuition — the necessary conditions under which anything can appear to us at all. We do not discover space and time in the world; rather, they are the framework our minds impose on all possible experience. This is why geometry and arithmetic yield synthetic a priori knowledge: they describe the structure of our own intuitive framework, which all experience must necessarily conform to.

At the level of the understanding, the categories — causality, substance, unity, plurality, and others — are the pure concepts through which the mind organizes the raw data of sensory experience into coherent, objective knowledge. Without the categories, experience would be nothing but a blind, buzzing manifold of sensation. Without sensory intuition, the categories would be empty forms with no content. Thoughts without content are empty; intuitions without concepts are blind.

This framework yields a powerful result: synthetic a priori knowledge is possible, but only of objects as they appear to us (phenomena), never of things as they are in themselves (noumena). We can know with certainty that every event in our experience has a cause, because causality is one of the conditions our mind imposes on experience. But we cannot know whether things in themselves, independent of our cognitive apparatus, are governed by causality.

The consequence for traditional metaphysics is devastating. Reason, operating beyond the bounds of possible experience, falls into irresolvable contradictions — the antinomies — when it attempts to prove or disprove the existence of God, the freedom of the will, or the immortality of the soul. These questions cannot be settled by theoretical reason. Kant thus limits knowledge to make room for faith, drawing a boundary that reshapes the scope of philosophy itself.
