# Novum Organum — Francis Bacon

> Excerpt from Novum Organum Scientiarum, published 1620. Public domain.

Bacon's project is nothing less than the renovation of human knowledge. The current state of the sciences is unsatisfactory — centuries of effort have yielded little of practical value because inquiry has been conducted by faulty methods. The Aristotelian logic of the syllogism is useless for the discovery of new truths; it merely rearranges what is already believed. What is needed is a new instrument — a novum organum — for the interpretation of nature, one that proceeds from careful observation through graduated induction to general principles.

But before the new method can be applied, the mind must be cleared of the systematic errors that distort its perception of reality. Bacon identifies four classes of these errors, which he calls Idols — false notions that have taken up residence in the human understanding, obstructing the entry of truth.

The Idols of the Tribe are rooted in human nature itself and belong to the entire race. The human understanding is like a false mirror, which, receiving rays irregularly, distorts and discolours the nature of things by mingling its own nature with it. The mind is prone to suppose more order and regularity in nature than it actually finds. It seizes upon affirmative instances and neglects negative ones. It is moved by the sudden and striking while ignoring the gradual and ordinary. It is impatient and leaps to conclusions from insufficient evidence. These tendencies are inherent in the very structure of human cognition and require constant vigilance to counteract.

The Idols of the Cave are the peculiar errors of each individual, arising from that person's unique nature, education, habits, and circumstances. Each man has a cave or den of his own, which refracts and discolours the light of nature. Some minds are attracted to novelty, others to antiquity. Some attend to differences, others to resemblances. Some are best suited to the contemplation of particulars, others to generalising. The individual's temperament, training, and experience create personal biases that systematically distort his understanding of the world.

The Idols of the Marketplace arise from the commerce and association of men with each other through language. Words are formed according to the apprehension of the vulgar and impose themselves upon the understanding. When a sharper intellect or more diligent observation tries to draw the lines of nature more accurately, words stand in the way. Some words name things that do not exist. Others, applied to things that do exist, are confused and ill-defined, derived from a narrow set of cases that do not capture the full range of the phenomenon. The ill and unfit choice of words wonderfully obstructs the understanding, and words plainly force and overrule the understanding, throwing all into confusion.

The Idols of the Theatre are the received systems of philosophy, which Bacon compares to so many stage plays, representing worlds of their own creation in an unreal and scenic fashion. Every philosophical system that has been received or invented is like a play — an artificial construction that imposes its plot on reality rather than learning from it. Bacon criticises the rationalist method that builds elaborate systems from a few axioms, the empirical method that generalises rashly from a narrow set of experiments, and the superstitious method that corrupts philosophy with theology and tradition.

Bacon's doctrine of the Idols is not merely a catalogue of errors but a prolegomenon to method. Until the mind is freed from these systematic distortions, no amount of observation or reasoning will yield reliable knowledge. The first step in the advancement of learning is the purgation of the intellect — an honest reckoning with the sources of human error that clears the way for the patient, methodical investigation of nature.
