# Democracy in America — Alexis de Tocqueville

> Excerpt from De la democratie en Amerique, published 1835. Public domain.

Tocqueville identifies what he considers the most formidable danger inherent in democratic societies: the tyranny of the majority. In a democracy, the majority possesses a degree of moral and political authority unlike anything found in aristocratic nations. The moral authority rests on the principle that the interests of the many are to be preferred to those of the few. The political authority derives from the fact that in democratic nations, the majority literally holds sovereign power through the mechanisms of popular election and legislative control.

The consequence is that the majority in the United States commands an authority that is nearly irresistible. It directs the legislature, executes the laws, controls public opinion, and reaches into the private conscience of citizens. Tocqueville finds no comparable concentration of power in any other form of government. In aristocracies, the sovereign must contend with an independent nobility. In monarchies, the king may be checked by the church, the courts, or regional authorities. In America, the majority faces no comparable countervailing force. When the majority has once irrevocably decided a question, all discussion ceases, and everyone, including its opponents, submits silently to its decree.

This domination extends beyond legal and institutional channels to the realm of thought itself. Tocqueville argues that the majority exercises a power over opinion that is more absolute than any religious or political authority in Europe. In democratic America, the majority draws a formidable circle around thought. Within those limits, the writer is free; but woe to him who dares to transgress them. He will not face the inquisition, but he will be shunned, denied influence, and effectively silenced. The tyranny of the majority does not use the stake and the scaffold; it employs ostracism and social death.

Tocqueville contrasts this with older forms of tyranny that controlled the body but left the soul free. Democratic despotism reverses the formula. It leaves the body free but enchains the soul. The dissenter retains his civil rights but finds them useless. He may remain among his fellow citizens, but they treat him as an impure being. He is abandoned by his friends, shunned by his acquaintances, and finds that even those who believe he is right lack the courage to say so publicly. This power over social opinion is, in Tocqueville's judgment, more penetrating and durable than any crude exercise of physical force.

Yet Tocqueville does not conclude that democracy is therefore hopeless. He identifies several features of American life that mitigate the danger. The federal structure divides sovereignty between national and state governments, preventing any single majority from exercising total control. The independence of the judiciary, and especially the power of judicial review, provides a check on legislative overreach. The legal profession acts as a kind of informal aristocracy, instilling respect for precedent, process, and the rights of individuals. The practice of local self-government in townships and counties habituates citizens to the exercise of liberty and creates multiple centres of power and loyalty.

Above all, the spirit of association in America creates a dense network of voluntary organizations — religious, civic, commercial, and political — that stand between the isolated individual and the omnipotent state. These associations enable minorities to make their voices heard, provide alternative centres of authority and loyalty, and prevent the atomization of society into a mass of disconnected individuals facing an overwhelming majority alone. The vitality of these intermediate institutions is, for Tocqueville, the essential condition for the survival of liberty within a democratic order.
