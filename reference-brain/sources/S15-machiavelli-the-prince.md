# The Prince — Niccolo Machiavelli

> Excerpt from Il Principe, published 1532. Public domain.

Machiavelli's central concern is the acquisition and maintenance of political power in conditions where moral idealism is a recipe for ruin. He announces at the outset that he intends to write something useful to one who understands, and therefore it appears to him more fitting to go directly to the effectual truth of the thing than to the imagination of it. Many have imagined republics and principalities that have never been seen or known to exist in reality. A man who wishes to act entirely according to his professions of virtue will soon find himself ruined among so many who are not virtuous. Therefore, a prince who wishes to maintain himself must learn how not to be good, and use or refrain from using that ability according to necessity.

On the question of whether it is better to be loved or feared, Machiavelli offers his famous answer: one would wish to be both, but since it is difficult to unite them in one person, it is much safer to be feared than loved. Love is preserved by a bond of obligation which, owing to the baseness of men, is broken at every opportunity for their advantage. But fear preserves the prince through a dread of punishment that never fails. The prince should, however, make himself feared in such a way that if he does not win love, he at least avoids hatred. This he can always do so long as he abstains from the property and women of his citizens and subjects.

Machiavelli extends this realism to the question of keeping faith. A prince should appear to be all mercy, all fidelity, all humanity, all integrity, all religion. But he must have a mind disposed to adapt according to the wind of fortune and the variations of circumstances. He must be prepared not to be good if constrained by necessity. Men judge more by the eyes than by the hands: everyone sees what you appear to be, few experience what you really are. The prince who succeeds in his enterprises will always be judged honourable.

On the role of fortune, Machiavelli argues that fortune governs roughly half of human affairs but leaves the other half to human control and initiative. He compares fortune to a river in flood, which destroys everything before it when no provision has been made. But where dykes and embankments are built in quiet times, the river may be directed and its force managed. The prince who relies entirely on fortune is ruined when it changes. The one who adapts his conduct to the character of the times prospers.

Machiavelli insists on the distinction between cruelty well used and cruelty badly used. Cruelties well used are those exercised all at once, out of necessity to secure the state, and then not persisted in but turned to the greatest possible benefit of the subjects. Cruelties badly used are those that, though few in the beginning, multiply with time rather than diminishing. He who seizes a state should calculate all the injuries he must inflict and deliver them all at a stroke, so as not to have to repeat them daily. Benefits, by contrast, should be conferred gradually so that they are better savoured.

The overall teaching is that political survival demands a willingness to act outside conventional morality when circumstances require it, combined with sufficient prudence and skill to maintain the appearance of virtue. Machiavelli does not celebrate cruelty or deception for their own sake, but insists that the statesman who refuses to employ them when necessary will lose his state, and with it any capacity to achieve good.
