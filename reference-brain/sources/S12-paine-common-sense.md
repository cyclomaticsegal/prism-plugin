# Common Sense — Thomas Paine

> Excerpt from Common Sense, published 1776. Public domain.

Paine opens by drawing a sharp distinction between society and government. Society is produced by our wants, and government by our wickedness. Society in every state is a blessing, but government, even in its best state, is a necessary evil, and in its worst state an intolerable one. Society encourages intercourse and is a positive force. Government restrains vices and is at best a negative force. When we suffer or are exposed to the same miseries by a government that we might expect in a country without government, our calamity is heightened by reflecting that we furnish the means by which we suffer.

Paine proceeds to demolish the legitimacy of monarchical government and hereditary succession. The distinction of men into kings and subjects is not a natural one. In the early ages of the world, there were no kings, and as a consequence there were no wars. The heathens were the first to introduce government by kings, and it was the most prosperous invention the devil ever set on foot for the promotion of idolatry. The system of monarchy traces its origins to nothing better than the principal ruffian of some restless gang, whose savage manners obtained him the title of chief among plunderers. England's own monarchy sprang from William the Conqueror, a French bastard landing with an armed banditti, and establishing himself king of England against the consent of the natives.

Hereditary succession, Paine argues, is an absurdity compounded upon an evil. Even if the first king had earned his position through some merit, nature does not guarantee that his descendants will share those qualities. Hereditary right opens the door to the foolish, the wicked, and the improper. It makes men perpetual rulers who never merited the position and who grow insolent, detached from the concerns of their subjects, and unfit for their duties. The evil of monarchy is not merely that a single person governs, but that the mechanism of succession prevents the governed from correcting the defect.

Turning to the specific situation of the American colonies, Paine argues that reconciliation with Britain is neither possible nor desirable. The advocates of reconciliation ask: has not America flourished under her connection with Britain? Paine answers that America would have flourished as much, and probably much more, had no European power taken any notice of her. The commerce by which she enriched herself was with the whole of Europe, not merely with Britain, and would have continued regardless. Britain has protected America not from affection but from interest, for the sake of trade and dominion, and would protect Turkey from the same motive.

The injuries and disadvantages of the connection are without number. Any submission to or dependence on Britain tends directly to involve the continent in European wars and quarrels and sets it at variance with nations who would otherwise seek friendship. France and Spain were never enemies of America in her capacity as Americans, only as subjects of Britain.

Paine's most powerful argument concerns the practical absurdity of a continent being perpetually governed by an island. In no instance has nature made the satellite larger than its primary planet. England and America reverse the common order of nature. The authority of Britain over America is a form of government which sooner or later must have an end. The blood of the slain, the weeping voice of nature cries: it is time to part. Even the distance at which the Almighty has placed England and America is a strong and natural proof that the authority of the one over the other was never the design of heaven.
