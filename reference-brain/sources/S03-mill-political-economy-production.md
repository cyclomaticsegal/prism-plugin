# Principles of Political Economy — John Stuart Mill

> Excerpt from Principles of Political Economy with Some of Their Applications to Social Philosophy, published 1848. Public domain.

Mill opens his treatment of production by distinguishing it sharply from distribution. The laws and conditions of the production of wealth partake of the character of physical truths. There is nothing optional or arbitrary in them. Whether human labour and ingenuity can produce more or less depends on the physical properties of materials, the state of knowledge, and the efficiency of cooperative arrangements. These are matters of fact, not of opinion. The distribution of wealth, by contrast, is a matter of human institution. The rules by which it is determined are what the opinions and feelings of the ruling portion of the community make them, and are very different in different ages and countries.

This distinction is fundamental to Mill's project. It allows him to accept the classical economists' analysis of production — the roles of labour, capital, and land — while insisting that society has wide latitude in how it distributes what is produced. The implication is reformist: even if production follows iron laws, the social arrangement of its fruits is open to deliberate redesign.

Mill identifies three requisites of production: labour, natural agents, and capital. Labour encompasses all human exertion directed toward producing useful things, including mental effort and the labour of superintendence and direction. Mill emphasizes that unproductive labour is not synonymous with useless labour. A musician's performance is unproductive in the economic sense — it does not result in a material commodity — yet it may be of the highest social value. The distinction matters only for the analysis of wealth accumulation, not for moral judgments about worth.

Natural agents — land, water, minerals, climate — constitute the second requisite. Mill follows Ricardo in recognizing that land of varying quality produces varying returns, and that natural advantages confer differential rents. But he is more attentive than Ricardo to the ways in which human knowledge can alter what counts as a natural advantage. Lands that were barren become fertile through drainage, irrigation, and scientific agriculture. The boundary between nature's contribution and humanity's is not fixed.

Capital, the third requisite, Mill defines as the accumulated stock of the produce of previous labour, set aside to assist current production. He stresses that capital is not identical with money. The capital of a country consists of all the food, clothing, tools, raw materials, and machinery that are used to support labourers and facilitate their work. Money is merely the mechanism by which capital is transferred and allocated.

Mill devotes particular attention to the role of capital in supporting labour during the interval between the beginning of production and the completion of a saleable product. The labourer cannot wait for his corn to grow or his cloth to be woven before eating. Capital advances subsistence to the labourer, enabling the long and indirect processes of production that are vastly more efficient than hand-to-mouth methods. The extent to which a society can employ roundabout, capital-intensive methods of production is therefore limited by the stock of capital available.

On the increase of production, Mill identifies two chief sources: the increase of labour and the increase of capital. But he adds a third that he considers ultimately more important — improvements in the knowledge and skill with which labour and capital are applied. Technological progress, better methods of organization, advances in science applied to agriculture and manufactures, and the extension of education to the working population all raise the productivity of existing resources. Mill argues that there is no practical limit to the improvements that human ingenuity may yet achieve in extracting more output from the same inputs, a view that tempers the Malthusian pessimism he otherwise partly accepts.
