# Nicomachean Ethics — Aristotle

> Excerpt from Nicomachean Ethics, Book I (On the Good), circa 340 BCE. Public domain.

Every art and every inquiry, and similarly every action and pursuit, is thought to aim at some good; and for this reason the good has rightly been declared to be that at which all things aim. Aristotle opens the Ethics with this teleological observation, establishing that all human activity is directed toward some end perceived as good. But goods differ, and they are ordered hierarchically. Some goods are pursued for the sake of others — wealth for the sake of comfort, medicine for the sake of health. There must therefore be some ultimate good that is desired for its own sake and not for the sake of anything beyond it. This supreme good, the end to which all other ends are subordinate, is what Aristotle calls eudaimonia — happiness, or more precisely, human flourishing.

Both the many and the cultivated agree verbally that the highest good is happiness, but they disagree about what happiness consists in. The common run of people identify it with pleasure, wealth, or honour. Aristotle examines and rejects each of these candidates. Pleasure is the life of grazing animals, unworthy of truly human aspiration. Wealth is merely useful — it is always pursued for the sake of something else. Honour depends on those who bestow it rather than on the person who receives it, and is therefore too superficial and unstable to constitute the good.

Aristotle's method is to work from the function argument. Just as the good of a flute player lies in playing the flute well, and the good of a sculptor lies in sculpting well, the good of a human being must lie in performing well the function distinctive to human beings. What is this distinctive function? It cannot be mere living, which we share with plants, nor sensation, which we share with animals. It must be the active exercise of the soul's faculties in conformity with rational principle — or, if there are several virtues, in conformity with the best and most complete virtue, and moreover in a complete life. One swallow does not make a summer, nor does one day, and so too one day or a brief time does not make a man blessed and happy.

Happiness thus requires both virtue and a sufficient span of time in which to exercise it. Aristotle further argues that it requires a measure of external goods — health, friends, moderate wealth, political stability. A man who is utterly destitute, or who suffers terrible misfortunes, cannot be called happy in the full sense, however virtuous his character. This is not because happiness is identical with good fortune, but because the exercise of virtue requires materials and opportunities that extreme deprivation removes.

Aristotle distinguishes between intellectual virtues and moral virtues. Intellectual virtues — wisdom, understanding, prudence — are developed primarily through teaching and require experience and time. Moral virtues — courage, temperance, justice, generosity — are acquired through habituation. We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts. Virtue is not merely a feeling or a capacity but a settled disposition to choose the mean between excess and deficiency in action and emotion, as determined by the standard that a person of practical wisdom would apply.

The opening book thus establishes the framework for the entire Ethics: the good for a human being is an activity of soul in accordance with the highest virtue, sustained over a complete life, supported by adequate external conditions. This is not a static condition but an ongoing activity — the lifelong practice of living well and doing well.
