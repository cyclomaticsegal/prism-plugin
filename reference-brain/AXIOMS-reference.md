# AXIOMS — Reference Brain

## The Prism

> The history of human progress is the history of individuals and institutions discovering how to organise knowledge, labour, and power — and every great disruption occurs when a new method of organisation displaces the old.

---

## Frameworks

### Framework 1: The Division of Knowledge Parallels the Division of Labour

**Claim:** Just as Smith showed that dividing physical labour creates exponential productivity gains, the division of intellectual labour — specialisation of knowledge domains — drives progress. But it also creates fragmentation. The thinkers who bridge specialisations (Bacon, Franklin, Mill) produce disproportionate insight.

**Anchored by:** C01 (Division of Labour, from S01), C08 (Bacon's Scientific Method, from S18), C14 (Franklin's Self-Improvement System, from S26). Smith describes physical specialisation; Bacon describes intellectual specialisation (the idols that fragment thinking); Franklin practices cross-domain synthesis.

**Domain(s):** Economics (1), Innovation (6), Epistemology (2)

**Boundary:** Breaks down when specialisation produces incommensurable paradigms — when experts in different domains literally cannot understand each other's frameworks. Kuhn's paradigm incommensurability (not in this corpus but implied by the tension between Bacon and Descartes).

---

### Framework 2: Legitimate Power Requires Institutional Constraints

**Claim:** Every political theorist in this corpus — Locke, Madison, Machiavelli, Tocqueville — grapples with the same problem: how to prevent power from becoming tyrannical. The answer is always institutional design, never individual virtue.

**Anchored by:** C05 (Locke's Property Rights, from S11), C06 (Madison's Faction Theory, from S13), C07 (Tocqueville's Tyranny of Majority, from S14), C09 (Machiavelli's Political Realism, from S15). Locke constraints via natural rights; Madison via faction balancing; Tocqueville via civil associations; Machiavelli via strategic necessity.

**Domain(s):** Governance (3), Ethics (7)

**Boundary:** Fails in contexts where institutions themselves are captured or where the population lacks the civic infrastructure Tocqueville describes. Machiavelli's realism suggests institutions are only as strong as the power dynamics sustaining them.

---

### Framework 3: The Moral Status of Productivity

**Claim:** From Smith through Carnegie, there is a persistent assumption that productive activity is morally good — that wealth creation benefits society. This assumption is challenged by Marx (exploitation), Thoreau (simplicity), and Douglass (whose labour was stolen, not exchanged).

**Anchored by:** C01 (Division of Labour, from S01), C03 (Marx's Labour Theory of Value, from S04), C11 (Thoreau's Economy of Simplicity, from S23), C10 (Douglass on Literacy as Liberation, from S21), C13 (Carnegie's Gospel of Wealth, from S27).

**Domain(s):** Economics (1), Ethics (7), Culture & Society (5)

**Boundary:** The framework assumes a context of voluntary exchange. When labour is coerced (Douglass) or when productivity destroys non-market goods (Thoreau's nature), the moral calculus inverts.

---

### Framework 4: Empiricism vs Rationalism as Competing Operating Systems

**Claim:** The Descartes-Hume-Kant sequence is not just a philosophical debate — it's a template for how any field resolves the tension between top-down theory and bottom-up evidence. Science (Bacon, Newton, Darwin) chose empiricism. Ethics (Kant, Mill) remains split.

**Anchored by:** C04 (Descartes' Cogito, from S08), C15 (Hume's Problem of Induction, from S09), C16 (Kant's Synthetic A Priori, from S10), C08 (Bacon's Idols, from S18), C12 (Darwin's Natural Selection, from S16).

**Domain(s):** Epistemology (2), Science (4), Ethics (7)

**Boundary:** Kant's synthesis (experience provides content, reason provides structure) is elegant but doesn't resolve practical disagreements about which framework to apply in specific cases.

## Cross-Domain Bridges

### Bridge 1: Labour, Knowledge, and Power

**Connects:** Economics (1) <-> Governance (3) <-> Epistemology (2)

**The link:** Smith's division of labour, Bacon's scientific method, and Madison's institutional design all address the same problem: how to organise collective human effort to produce outcomes no individual could achieve alone. The economic, epistemic, and political solutions are structurally parallel.

**Evidence:** C01, C06, C08 — all describe systems where individual limitations are overcome through structured collaboration.

---

### Bridge 2: Individual vs Collective

**Connects:** Culture & Society (5) <-> Ethics (7) <-> Economics (1)

**The link:** Emerson's self-reliance, Mill's utilitarianism, and Smith's invisible hand represent three different answers to: should the individual optimise for themselves or for the group? Emerson says self; Mill says group; Smith says self-interest accidentally serves the group.

**Evidence:** C24 (Emerson, S24), C17 (Mill's Utility, S29), C01 (Smith, S01).

---

### Bridge 3: Evidence and Authority

**Connects:** Science (4) <-> Governance (3) <-> Epistemology (2)

**The link:** Galileo's confrontation with Church authority, Bacon's rejection of received wisdom, and Paine's rejection of monarchical authority all challenge the same structure: argument from authority. The scientific revolution and the democratic revolution share an epistemic root.

**Evidence:** C19 (Galileo, S19), C08 (Bacon, S18), C20 (Paine, S12).
